1945 – Picking Up the Pace

On July 16, 1945, the United States conducted the first ever test, called the Trinity Test, of a nuclear weapon. Shortly thereafter, Churchill, Stalin, and Truman met at the Potsdam Conference to discuss the fate of postwar Europe, and to issue a call for an unconditional Japanese surrender. At this meeting, Truman told Stalin of “a new weapon of unusual destructive force.”

The Potsdam conference ended on August 2, 1945. On August 6, the United States dropped “Little Boy,” a plutonium bomb, on Hiroshima. On August 9, the United States dropped “Fat Man,” another atomic weapon, on Nagasaki. By August 15, Emperor Hirohito announced Japan’s surrender.

By August 20, Stalin had realized the incredible importance of nuclear weapons and sent a letter calling for the creation of a special committee to oversee nuclear bomb development. Members of this committee included Lavrenti Beria and Igor Kurchatov. In this letter, Stalin also instructed Lavrenti Beria “to take measures aimed at organizing foreign intelligence work to gain more complete technical and economic information about the uranium industry and about atomic bombs.”
